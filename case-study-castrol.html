The Situation

A brand people knew — but didn't choose

Motor oil is one of the lowest-engagement categories in retail. Customers walk in knowing what they want and walk out in under a minute. Castrol had awareness but not consideration — shoppers saw it as a "dad's brand" and defaulted to whatever was cheapest or most familiar.

BP needed a way to re-introduce Castrol to DIY enthusiasts at Walmart, the single largest motor oil retail channel. The challenge wasn't media spend — it was creating something worth stopping for in a category nobody stops for.

The Strategy

Meet DIYers where they're already passionate

The insight was simple: DIY motor oil buyers don't just change oil. They're high-engagement shoppers across multiple categories — home improvement tools, workout equipment, and storage/organization all over-indexed with this audience.

Instead of fighting for attention in the oil aisle, the strategy was to build a cross-category destination that felt native to how DIYers actually shop. Castrol would be the host, not the pitch — curating products from across Walmart's catalog under one shoppable hub, anchored by former NFL star Larry Fitzgerald as the face of the experience.

The core concept: Larry's Garage — a fully shoppable, 360° interactive destination on Walmart.com where shoppers could explore a virtual garage, watch how-to videos, browse cross-category products, and add to cart without ever leaving the experience.

What We Built

A 360° retail media experience — from zero

This was the first experience of its kind on Walmart.com. There was no template, no existing playbook, no precedent for what a shoppable branded destination could look like inside Walmart's ecosystem. Everything had to be program-managed from scratch across creative, sales, production, and Walmart's own platform teams.

The Larry's Garage hub live on Walmart.com — a 360° shoppable destination, first of its kind on the platform.
  • A fully interactive, 360° shoppable hub on Walmart.com featuring Larry Fitzgerald's garage with hotspot-driven product discovery
  • Coordinated cross-category merchandising pulling in products from home improvement, fitness, storage, and automotive
  • "How to" video content embedded directly in the experience, featuring Larry walking through DIY basics
  • Integrated oil finder tool allowing shoppers to match the right Castrol product to their vehicle
  • Category takeover ads, display (on-site and off-site), search ads, and a first-ever Spanish-language campaign on Walmart Connect
  • Earned organic placement on Walmart.com's homepage navigation — the only brand product to receive a featured shop

Every piece of content was utility-driven and educational. The hub wasn't a landing page with a buy button — it was a genuine shopping experience designed to hold attention in a category that normally gets none.

The Operational Reality

Why this project would normally fail

Multi-stakeholder retail media campaigns like this are where most creative operations break down. You're coordinating between a brand team (BP/Castrol), a retailer's media arm (Walmart Connect), a celebrity and their representation (Larry Fitzgerald), production teams for video and interactive content, the retailer's category merchandising team, and platform engineers who have to build something that's never existed before.

Every handoff is a failure point. Misaligned timelines between creative and media. Approval loops that stall for weeks. Platform constraints discovered mid-build. Talent availability windows that don't align with production schedules.

The reason this shipped — and shipped successfully — is because the program management held. Every stakeholder had clear ownership, every dependency was mapped, and every deliverable had a single accountable path from brief to launch.
